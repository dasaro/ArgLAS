\begin{abstract}
Argumentation semantics specify which arguments are acceptable in a framework of
conflicting claims. We present a unifying framework, based on Learning from
Answer Sets, that learns acceptability semantics across abstract, bipolar, and
--- via a corrected translation --- assumption-based argumentation, with a
unified background theory that also covers value-based frameworks, returning
interpretable encodings rather than black-box predictors. First, correctness is
\emph{proved}: the learned stable, admissible, and complete encodings are shown
equivalent to their manually engineered counterparts on \emph{every} framework
--- for abstract and, new, for bipolar and value-based frameworks --- the
preferred construction
is characterized exactly, and the analysis uncovered --- and repaired --- a
defect in a published assumption-based translation. Second, a $3{,}240$-run controlled
study maps recovery of four semantics under partial labels, label noise, sample
size, and class imbalance: recovery from clean data is near-perfect --- and
a post-hoc audit shows $61\%$ of the clean-run theories, $94\%$ among those
with a perfect test score, are exactly extension-equivalent to the target on
an exhaustive class of small frameworks --- label
noise is the binding constraint --- one whose damage more data buys back for
the runs that complete within the compute budget, at the price of orders-of-magnitude
longer training and, in the noisiest cells, timeouts --- imbalance
is immaterial, and the same learner recovers bipolar and translated
assumption-based semantics with an unchanged hypothesis space (preferred on
self-attacking frameworks is the one mapped boundary). Third, the
synthetic study doubles as a calibration instrument for behavioural data.
Fourth, applied to a published human judgement study under an adversarially
audited protocol, no learned theory beats the best catalogue semantics (CF2)
--- yet the learner recovers a legible acceptance policy outside the standard
extension-based catalogue (committal reinstatement and cycle caution, plus a candidate distrust of
heavily attacked arguments that the stimuli cannot yet decide), and a
directional,
multiplicity-corrected test finds no graph condition whose own theory beats one
shared policy --- with the pooled contrast significantly favouring the shared
policy. All results are
reproducible from a pinned repository.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Formal argumentation models defeasible reasoning as the interaction of
conflicting arguments. Given a framework of arguments and attacks, an
\emph{acceptability semantics} determines which arguments a rational reasoner
should accept~\citep{dung1995}. Four decades of research have produced a rich
catalogue of such semantics, a mature ecosystem of \emph{solvers} that compute
them~\citep{egly2010,iccma23}, and, more recently, machine-learning systems
that \emph{approximate} them~\citep{craandijkbex2020}. All of these take the
semantics as given. This paper studies the converse problem: can acceptability
semantics themselves be \emph{learned} from examples of accepted and rejected
arguments --- and can the result be trusted?

The question matters for two reasons. Practically, hand-engineering an encoding
for each semantics and each framework family is error-prone --- we exhibit, in
Section~\ref{sec:mech}, a defect in a published translation that survived peer
review precisely because its correctness was argued by example. Scientifically,
whenever the target semantics is \emph{unknown} --- most vividly, when the
``reasoner'' is a human whose acceptance behaviour matches no catalogue entry
exactly --- a learner that returns an \emph{interpretable} theory is the only
instrument that can tell us \emph{what} principles are being followed, rather
than merely which catalogue entry fits least badly.

We answer four questions.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=2pt]
\item[Q1 (Learnable?)] Can a single Learning-from-Answer-Sets (LAS) setup, with
one background theory and one hypothesis space, learn the standard acceptability
semantics across abstract (AAF), bipolar (BAF), value-based (VAF), and
assumption-based (ABA) frameworks?
\item[Q2 (Correct?)] Are the learned programs \emph{provably} equivalent to the
reference encodings --- not on a test set, but on \emph{every} framework?
\item[Q3 (Robust?)] What happens when the examples are no longer clean and
hand-picked, but sampled, incomplete, noisy, and imbalanced --- as any
application to real data would produce?
\item[Q4 (Useful when the target is unknown?)] Applied to human judgement data,
does the learner recover a meaningful acceptance policy --- and does it tell us
something the best-fit catalogue comparison cannot?
\end{description}

\noindent Our contributions, in the order the paper develops them:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.},leftmargin=2.5em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{A unifying LAS framework} (Section~\ref{sec:framework}) for
learning acceptability semantics across AAF, BAF, VAF and ABA, with a single
background theory and hypothesis space that specializes to each family
(Remark~\ref{rem:spec}), and a \emph{corrected} ABA-to-AAF translation.
\item \textbf{Correctness proofs} (Section~\ref{sec:mech}). Equivalence
theorems between learned and manually engineered encodings: for the AAF
stable, admissible, and complete encodings on \emph{every} framework
(Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}), and --- new, to our knowledge --- for their bipolar
and value-based counterparts under support-extended and preference-disarmed
defeat (Theorems~\ref{thm:baf} and~\ref{thm:vaf}), with the recursive
closure instantiations recovered by splitting arguments
(Corollaries~\ref{cor:closure} and~\ref{cor:vafclosure}). All proofs go through a single classical route
--- both programs are tight, so by Fages' theorem their answer sets are the
models of their Clark completions, and the completions are shown to have
the same models on the shared vocabulary --- with full details in
\ref{app:proofs}.
\item \textbf{A corrected ABA-to-AAF translation} (Section~\ref{sec:abadefect}).
The published argument-construction step is incorrect: arguments are silently
dropped whenever any conclusion is derivable without assumptions. We give a
minimal counterexample and a corrected, definition-faithful construction.
\item \textbf{Preferred semantics, learnable at scale}
(Section~\ref{sec:prf}). A proof that subset-maximal selection over a learned
admissible (or complete) core computes exactly the preferred extensions
(Theorem~\ref{thm:prf}), plus the formulation that makes preferred learnable
through a realistic pipeline.
\item \textbf{Learnability under imperfect data} (Section~\ref{sec:exp1}). A
controlled recovery study --- four semantics $\times$ label completeness
$\times$ label noise $\times$ sample size $\times$ class proportions, $3{,}240$
learning runs under grouped cross-validation with a complete-information test
surface and a first-class failure taxonomy --- plus breadth experiments over a
sparser generator and over BAF and translated-ABA inputs.
\item \textbf{A calibrated bridge to behavioural data}
(Section~\ref{sec:bridge}). The synthetic study validates oracle-free negative
examples, gates every behavioural pipeline on clean textbook labels, and
licenses a decomposition of human-data error into recovery limit versus model
mismatch.
\item \textbf{The semantics people follow} (Sections~\ref{sec:exp2}
and~\ref{sec:humansem}). An adversarially audited re-analysis of the
\citet{guillaume2022} human study: learned theories do not beat CF2 (confirming
the original finding under a stronger test); the learner nevertheless recovers a
legible human acceptance policy --- committal reinstatement and cycle
caution, recurring in $15$--$20$ of $20$ cross-seed folds, plus a
lower-stability candidate hedge (distrust of heavily attacked arguments)
aligned with graded semantics --- whose audited principle profile matches no
semantics in the extension-based catalogue;
psychologically motivated features close roughly a third of the in-corpus
gap to CF2 (a descriptive gain that does not transfer to held-out
conditions); and
a directional, multiplicity-corrected test finds no support for per-graph
semantics, with the pooled contrast significantly favouring one
structure-sensitive policy.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Argumentation frameworks and their semantics}
\label{sec:bg-af}

An \emph{abstract argumentation framework} (AAF)~\citep{dung1995} is a pair
$F=(A,R)$ with $A$ a finite set of arguments and $R\subseteq A\times A$ an
attack relation. A \emph{labelling}~\citep{caminada2006} assigns each argument
one of $\{\pred{in},\pred{out},\pred{undec}\}$; an extension-based semantics
corresponds to the set of arguments labelled \pred{in}. Argument $X$ is
\emph{defeated} (w.r.t.\ a set $S$ of accepted arguments) if some $Y\in S$
attacks $X$, and \emph{defended} if every attacker of $X$ is defeated. A set
$S$ is \emph{conflict-free} if no member attacks another; \emph{admissible} if
conflict-free and self-defending; \emph{complete} if admissible and containing
every argument it defends; \emph{stable} if conflict-free and attacking every
outside argument; \emph{preferred} if subset-maximal admissible; and
\emph{grounded} if subset-minimal complete~\citep{baroni2011}. The
SCC-recursive semantics CF2~\citep{baroni2005}, which treats odd cycles more
symmetrically, plays a role in Section~\ref{sec:exp2} as the best known
descriptor of human judgements. This paper's learnability study
(Section~\ref{sec:exp1}) covers the stable, admissible, complete, and preferred
semantics.\footnote{Grounded semantics is deliberately out of scope for the
learnability study, for a formulational reason: grounded is a functional
(single-extension, subset-minimal) target, and the campaign's single unified
guess-and-check learning background can only express constraint-prunable
semantics --- subset-minimality is not among them. On dense random frameworks
the grounded extension is additionally empty roughly $68\%$ of the time,
further starving positive signal. \ref{app:grd} closes the loop: with a
definite learning background, the identical harness recovers grounded exactly
(recovery $1.000$ at $f\ge20$ on sparse and dense pools alike, and already at
$f{=}10$ on the dense pool).}

Three extensions of AAFs appear in this paper. \emph{Bipolar} frameworks
(BAF)~\citep{cayrol2009} add a support relation; following the
support-extended-defeat reading, $X$ defeats $Y$ if $X$ attacks $Y$, or $X$
supports an attacker of $Y$ (supported attack), or $X$ attacks a supporter of
$Y$ (indirect attack), with support closed under transitivity.
\emph{Value-based} frameworks (VAF)~\citep{benchcapon2003} label arguments with
values and disarm attacks against preferred values. \emph{Assumption-based
argumentation} (ABA)~\citep{bondarenko1997} generates arguments as deductions
from assumptions: in a flat framework $(\mathcal L,\mathcal R,\mathcal A,\;
\bar{\cdot}\,)$, an argument is a pair $(s,\Sigma)$ of a claim $s$ derivable
from a subset-minimal support $\Sigma\subseteq\mathcal A$, and $(s,\Sigma)$
attacks $(t,\Delta)$ iff $s=\bar a$ for some $a\in\Delta$.

\subsection{Learning from Answer Sets}
\label{sec:bg-las}

Answer Set Programming (ASP)~\citep{gelfondkahl2014,gebser2019} is the host
formalism both for the reference encodings --- we use the ASPARTIX
family~\citep{egly2010} --- and for learning. \emph{Learning from Answer Sets}
(LAS)~\citep{law2016,law2019,law2018phd} induces an ASP program from examples:
a \emph{context-dependent partial interpretation} (CDPI) example is a pair of a
partial interpretation (atoms to include/exclude) and a context program (here:
the $\pred{arg}/\pred{att}$ facts of one framework). A hypothesis $H$
\emph{covers} a positive example if some answer set of
$B\cup H\cup\text{context}$ extends the partial interpretation, and must admit
no such answer set for negative examples. The hypothesis space is generated
from \emph{mode declarations}; ILASP~\citep{law2016} searches it for a
minimal-cost hypothesis, supporting \emph{noisy} learning by attaching penalties
to examples that may be left uncovered~\citep{law2018}: an example annotated
with weight $w$ contributes $w$ to the objective if uncovered, so a
sufficiently supported theory can out-vote corrupted examples.
FastLAS~\citep{law2020} trades some expressivity for scalability and is used in
Sections~\ref{sec:exp2} and~\ref{sec:eval}.

One operation beyond plain answer-set computation is needed. For a program $P$
and a designated predicate $p$, write $\ASstar_p(P)$ for the answer sets of
$P$ whose $p$-projection is \emph{subset-maximal} among the $p$-projections of
all answer sets of $P$; modern ASP solvers compute this projection
natively~\citep{gebser2019}. The optimization is
subset-inclusion, \emph{not} cardinality --- a distinction essential for
correctness, since a maximum-cardinality admissible set is preferred but not
every preferred extension has maximum cardinality. Subset-maximal selection
over \pred{in} is used by the preferred construction (Theorem~\ref{thm:prf})
and, symmetrically on the learned and reference sides, in the evaluation
pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:exp1setup}).

\subsection{Proof technique: tightness and program completion}
\label{sec:bg-verif}

The correctness results of Section~\ref{sec:mech} all go through one classical
route. A ground logic program is \emph{tight} if its positive atom
dependency graph --- atoms as vertices, an edge from head to body atom for
every \emph{positive} body literal --- is acyclic; a sufficient condition,
met by every program we compare, is acyclicity of the predicate-level positive
dependency graph. For tight programs, answer sets
coincide with the models of the \emph{Clark
completion}~\citep{clark1978,fages1994,erdem2003}: the first-order theory that
replaces each predicate's defining rules by a biconditional (the predicate
holds iff one of its rule bodies does) and adds the program's constraints.
Every program compared in Section~\ref{sec:mech} is tight --- the only genuinely
recursive definition in the paper, the transitive closure of support, is
handled separately by the splitting theorem~\citep{lifschitz1994} in
Corollary~\ref{cor:closure}. Equivalence of two tight programs on a shared
input vocabulary therefore reduces to their completions having the same
models on that vocabulary, which can be established by ordinary propositional
reasoning; this is how all the proofs in \ref{app:proofs} proceed.

\section{A Unifying Framework for Learning Argumentation Semantics}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{One background, one hypothesis space}
\label{sec:unified}

The framework, LAS$_{\text{arg}}$, fixes a single background theory and mode
bias and lets only the \emph{examples} determine the semantics. Two ASP
contexts must be distinguished --- conflating them would make the correctness
statements of Section~\ref{sec:mech} false. The \emph{learning background}
$B_{\mathrm{AAF}}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:bg}) contains choice rules that make
labellings guessable, so that CDPI examples are meaningful; it is the theory
$B$ of the learning task. The \emph{definitional context} $\Daaf$
(Figure~\ref{fig:defs}) contains only the two derived-vocabulary definitions;
it is the context over which the equivalence theorems are stated and proved.

\noindent The mode bias allows $\pred{in}/\pred{out}$ in the head and the
derived vocabulary plus $\pred{arg}$, $\pred{att}$, $\pred{in}$, $\pred{out}$
in the body, with at most two variables. The unified background $B$ for all
four framework families extends $B_{\mathrm{AAF}}$ with support-extended defeat
(for BAF) and value-preference-disarmed defeat (for VAF).

\begin{remark}[Specialization]
\label{rem:spec}
When the extra input relations (\pred{support}, \pred{valpref}) are empty, $B$
specializes to $B_{\mathrm{AAF}}$ on the derived vocabulary
$\{\pred{in},\pred{out},\pred{defeated},\pred{not\_defended}\}$: the
support-closure rule and the two support-mediated defeat rules have
unsatisfiable bodies and derive nothing, and the preference-disarmed rule
$\pred{defeat}(X,Y)\leftarrow\pred{att}(X,Y),\ \neg\pred{pref}(Y,X)$ (where
\pred{pref} is the argument-level preference derived from \pred{valpref})
collapses to $\pred{defeat}(X,Y)\leftarrow\pred{att}(X,Y)$ --- so every such
rule either vanishes or reduces to its $B_{\mathrm{AAF}}$ counterpart,
immediately by inspection. The one deliberate exception is \pred{supported}/1,
which $B$ defines in the bipolar reading (Figure~\ref{fig:bgbaf}): with empty
\pred{support} it becomes empty rather than reverting to the AAF reading.
The unified and per-family presentations are therefore interchangeable for
hypotheses that do not mention \pred{supported} --- which includes every fixed
encoding and every program a correctness theorem is stated about (cf.\ the
caption of Figure~\ref{fig:bgbaf}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{From ABA to AAF, corrected}
\label{sec:abatranslation}

To learn semantics of ABA frameworks we translate them to AAFs and reuse the
machinery above. The translation constructs (i) the arguments --- all pairs
$(s,\Sigma)$ with $\Sigma$ a subset-minimal assumption support deriving claim
$s$ --- and (ii) the attacks --- $(s,\Sigma)$ attacks $(t,\Delta)$ iff
$s=\bar a$ for some $a\in\Delta$. Step (ii) is a single ASP rule and is
immediate from the definition. Step (i) is where a published enumeration
program is \emph{incorrect}; we present here the corrected construction and
defer the defect analysis to Section~\ref{sec:abadefect}. The corrected
step (i) performs one enumeration per candidate root $s$: require $s$ to be
derived, and enumerate the assumption sets that are subset-minimal among those
deriving $s$ (the projection $\ASstar$ of Section~\ref{sec:bg-las}, applied to
\pred{assume} with minimization in place of maximization).

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:aba}
For every flat ABA framework, the corrected step (i) returns exactly the
arguments $(s,\Sigma)$ of the definition.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix a derivable claim $s$. In the enumeration with $s$ required, the answer
sets correspond one-to-one with the assumption sets that derive $s$; the
subset-minimal projection over the assumption vocabulary retains precisely
the $\subseteq$-minimal such sets, i.e.\ the supports $\Sigma$ with
$(s,\Sigma)$ an argument. Ranging over all derivable $s$ yields all
arguments and nothing else.
\end{proof}

\section{Correctness of the Learned Encodings}
\label{sec:mech}

The programs of Figure~\ref{fig:learned} were \emph{learned}; nothing
guarantees a learner's output generalizes beyond its examples. This section
shows the learned encodings are not approximately right but \emph{exactly}
right: for stable, admissible, and complete --- on abstract, bipolar, and
value-based frameworks alike --- provably equivalent to the manually
engineered ASPARTIX-style encodings on \emph{every} framework
(Theorems~\ref{thm:aaf}, \ref{thm:baf} and~\ref{thm:vaf}), and for the
preferred construction provably correct via a
maximality characterization (Theorem~\ref{thm:prf}). All equivalences follow
one classical route (Section~\ref{sec:bg-verif}): both programs are tight, so
by Fages' theorem their answer sets are the models of their Clark completions,
and the two completions have the same models on the shared vocabulary. We
give the statements and
proof sketches here and the full completion computations in \ref{app:proofs}.
The value of insisting on proofs rather than spot checks is demonstrated
concretely by the defect analysed in Section~\ref{sec:abadefect}.

\subsection{Equivalence for abstract frameworks}
\label{sec:thm1}

Throughout, an AAF $F=(A,R)$ is encoded as the facts
$\{\pred{arg}(a): a\in A\}\cup\{\pred{att}(a,b):(a,b)\in R\}$; in particular
every domain element is an argument and
$\pred{att}\subseteq\pred{arg}\times\pred{arg}$.

\begin{theorem}[AAF equivalence]
\label{thm:aaf}
For each $\sigma\in\{\text{stable},\text{admissible},\text{complete}\}$ and
every AAF $F=(A,R)$, the programs $P_\sigma\cup \Daaf$ and the ASPARTIX
encoding $S_\sigma$ have the same answer sets restricted to
$\{\pred{in},\pred{out}\}$; the correspondence is a bijection (the remaining,
derived atoms are functionally determined by $\pred{in}$ and $\pred{att}$),
and the $\pred{in}$-projections are exactly the $\sigma$-extensions of $F$.
\end{theorem}

Note the theorem is stated over the definitional context $\Daaf$
(Figure~\ref{fig:defs}), not the learning background: with the choice rules of
$B_{\mathrm{AAF}}$ present, spurious guessed labellings would be answer sets
and the equivalence would fail (e.g.\ an unattacked argument could be guessed
\pred{out}).

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Neither $P_\sigma\cup\Daaf$ nor $S_\sigma$ contains positive recursion:
\pred{defeated} and \pred{not\_defended} depend positively only on input
facts and on \pred{in}; \pred{out} depends positively only on the derived
vocabulary (and \pred{arg}); \pred{in} depends positively only on
\pred{arg}; every remaining dependency is through negation. So all six
programs are tight and Fages' theorem applies:
their answer sets are exactly the models of their Clark completions. It then
suffices to show that the two completions have the same models on
$\{\pred{in},\pred{out}\}$. For stable, for instance, the completion of
$P_{\mathrm{stb}}\cup\Daaf$ says $\pred{in}(x)\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{out}(x)$
and $\pred{out}(x)\leftrightarrow\pred{defeated}(x)$, while the completion of
$S_{\mathrm{stb}}$ says $\pred{in}$ and $\pred{out}$ partition the arguments,
no two \pred{in} arguments attack one another, and every \pred{out} argument
is defeated; a short propositional argument (given in full in
\ref{app:proofs}, together with the admissible and complete cases) shows the
two theories have the same models on the shared vocabulary. In each direction
the key steps are that
$\pred{defeated}(x)\leftrightarrow\exists y\,
(\pred{att}(y,x)\wedge\pred{in}(y))$ is the same formula on both sides, and
that conflict-freeness is derivable from, respectively implies, the
fixpoint-style equations of the learned side.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The learner occasionally returns the variant stable program
\texttt{in(X) :- arg(X), not defeated(X)} in place of the second rule of
$P_{\mathrm{stb}}$. Its completion equation
$\pred{in}(x)\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{defeated}(x)$ is equivalent to the pair
$\pred{in}(x)\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{out}(x)$,
$\pred{out}(x)\leftrightarrow\pred{defeated}(x)$, so the same argument shows
the variant equivalent as well.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Equivalence for bipolar frameworks}
\label{sec:thm2}

The published account observed empirically that learned BAF programs agree
with guess-style reference encodings; no theorem was claimed. We close this
gap. The only new ingredient relative to Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf} is the
transitive support closure, a genuinely recursive definition that falls
outside the tight fragment; we handle it by first proving the equivalence with
the closure abstracted into an input relation, then recovering the recursive
instantiation by the splitting theorem.

\begin{theorem}[BAF equivalence]
\label{thm:baf}
Let $\mathit{supp}$ be \emph{any} relation satisfying
\[
\pred{support}\subseteq\mathit{supp}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\forall X,Y,Z\;\bigl(\mathit{supp}(X,Y)\wedge\pred{support}(Y,Z)\rightarrow
\mathit{supp}(X,Z)\bigr),
\]
and define defeat from $\mathit{supp}$ by the three defeat rules of
Figure~\ref{fig:bgbaf} (with \pred{support} replaced by $\mathit{supp}$ and the
closure rule removed). Then for each
$\sigma\in\{\text{stable},\text{admissible},\text{complete}\}$ the learned
program $P_\sigma$ (over these defeat rules and the $\Daaf$-style
\pred{defeated}/\pred{not\_defended} definitions reading \pred{defeat}) and
the guess-style reference encoding ($S_\sigma$ with \pred{att} replaced by
\pred{defeat} in its constraints and its \pred{defeated} rule, over the same
defeat rules) have the same answer sets restricted to
$\{\pred{in},\pred{out}\}$, for every input interpretation of
$\pred{arg}/1,\pred{att}/2,\pred{support}/2,\mathit{supp}/2$ in which, as in
Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}, every domain element is an argument (hence
$\pred{att},\pred{support},\mathit{supp}\subseteq
\pred{arg}\times\pred{arg}$).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
With $\mathit{supp}$ an input relation, \pred{defeat} is a non-recursive
defined predicate with the \emph{same} three defining rules on both sides, so
its completion equation is the same formula on both sides, and both programs
are tight. The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf} uses the attack relation only
through the completion equations of \pred{defeated} and \pred{not\_defended};
substituting \pred{defeat} for \pred{att} in those equations leaves the
propositional argument untouched. The equivalence therefore carries over
verbatim (details in \ref{app:proofs}). Inspection shows the two premises on
$\mathit{supp}$ are not used in this argument; they delimit the intended
instantiations, of which the transitive closure
(Corollary~\ref{cor:closure}) is the canonical one.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:closure}
The equivalence holds for the background as written in
Figure~\ref{fig:bgbaf}, i.e.\ at $\mathit{supp}=\pred{support}^{*}$, the
transitive closure computed in place by the recursive rule.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$\pred{support}^{*}$ satisfies both premises. It remains to show that the
recursive background and the background with $\pred{support}^{*}$ supplied as
facts have the same answer sets. The closure rule together with the
\pred{support} facts forms a positive bottom stratum whose atoms
(\pred{support}) do not occur in the heads of any other rule; by the splitting
theorem~\citep{lifschitz1994}, the answer sets of the whole program are
obtained by taking the unique answer set of the bottom --- the least model of a
positive program, which is exactly the transitive closure --- and evaluating
the remainder with those atoms fixed as facts. That remainder is the program
of Theorem~\ref{thm:baf} instantiated at $\mathit{supp}=\pred{support}^{*}$
(identifying the closed \pred{support} atoms with the input relation
$\mathit{supp}$, a renaming).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:baf} is \emph{stronger} than the claim
Corollary~\ref{cor:closure} needs: the equivalence holds for every relation
satisfying the premises --- in particular for every transitive superset of the
support relation, not only the closure itself --- a small bonus of proving the
statement in its abstracted form.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Equivalence for value-based frameworks}
\label{sec:thmvaf}

The value-based instantiation follows the same abstraction pattern. In the
unified background, the audience's value preference is derived and closed
transitively:

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{minipage}{0.72\linewidth}
\begin{verbatim}
valpref(X,Y) :- valpref(X,Z), valpref(Z,Y).
pref(X,Y)    :- valpref(U,V), val(X,U), val(Y,V).
pref(X,Y)    :- pref(X,Z), pref(Z,Y).
defeat(X,Y)  :- att(X,Y), not pref(Y,X).
\end{verbatim}
\end{minipage}
\caption{$B_{\mathrm{VAF}}$: transitive audience-preference closure and
preference-disarmed defeat, replacing the first rule of Figure~\ref{fig:bg};
\pred{defeated}/\pred{not\_defended} read \pred{defeat} as in
Figure~\ref{fig:bgbaf}.}
\label{fig:bgvaf}
\end{figure}

\begin{theorem}[VAF equivalence]
\label{thm:vaf}
Let \pred{pref} be \emph{any} relation, and define defeat by
$\pred{defeat}(X,Y)\leftarrow\pred{att}(X,Y),\ \neg\pred{pref}(Y,X)$. Then
for each $\sigma\in\{\text{stable},\text{admissible},\text{complete}\}$ the
learned program $P_\sigma$ (over this defeat rule and the $\Daaf$-style
definitions reading \pred{defeat}) and the guess-style reference encoding
($S_\sigma$ with \pred{att} replaced by \pred{defeat}, over the same defeat
rule) have the same answer sets restricted to $\{\pred{in},\pred{out}\}$, for
every input interpretation of
$\pred{arg}/1,\pred{att}/2,\pred{val}/2,\pred{valpref}/2,\pred{pref}/2$ in
which, as in Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}, every domain element is an argument.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Both sides contain the same single defining rule for \pred{defeat}, so both
completions contain the input-only equation
$\pred{defeat}(b,a)\leftrightarrow
\pred{att}(b,a)\wedge\neg\pred{pref}(a,b)$; the negative \pred{pref} literal
adds no edge to the positive dependency graph, so both programs remain tight.
As in Theorem~\ref{thm:baf}, the case proofs of Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf} use the
attack relation only through (D1), (D2), and (CF), and hence apply verbatim
with $\Att(a)=\{b:\pred{att}(b,a)\wedge\neg\pred{pref}(a,b)\}$
(\ref{app:proofs}).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:vafclosure}
The equivalence holds for the background as written in
Figure~\ref{fig:bgvaf}, i.e.\ with \pred{pref} derived from the audience's
value ordering and closed transitively in place.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Take as splitting set the \pred{val}, \pred{valpref}, and \pred{pref} atoms.
The rules with heads in this set --- the input facts and the three derivation
rules of Figure~\ref{fig:bgvaf} --- mention only atoms in the set, and no
other rule has a head in it; the bottom is a positive program whose unique
answer set derives exactly the closed preference. By the splitting
theorem~\citep{lifschitz1994}, the remainder is the program of
Theorem~\ref{thm:vaf} instantiated at that \pred{pref}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The semantic anchor is \citet{benchcapon2003}: for a fixed audience, $X$
defeats $Y$ iff $X$ attacks $Y$ and $Y$'s value is not preferred to $X$'s, so
$(A,\pred{defeat})$ is exactly Bench-Capon's audience-specific reduction and
the $\pred{in}$-projections are the audience-specific $\sigma$-extensions.
Applying the subset-maximal $\pred{in}$-selection of Theorem~\ref{thm:prf}
over the admissible core with the VAF defeat reading yields audience-specific
preferred extensions by the same argument. Unlike AAF and BAF, the VAF
instantiation carries no learning experiments in this paper
(Section~\ref{sec:concl}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Preferred semantics: correctness of the maximality construction}
\label{sec:prf}

Preferred extensions are subset-maximal admissible sets --- a second-order
property that no single tight program expresses, so the completion route of
Theorems~\ref{thm:aaf} and~\ref{thm:baf} does not apply directly to
constructions that reach preferred via the subset-maximal selection
$\ASstar$ of Section~\ref{sec:bg-las}. Correctness instead follows from
Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf} combined with a classical characterization of the
preferred semantics.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:prfmax}
For every finite AAF, the following families of sets coincide:
(a)~the preferred extensions; (b)~the $\subseteq$-maximal admissible sets;
(c)~the $\subseteq$-maximal complete extensions.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
(a)$=$(b) is the definition of preferred~\citep{dung1995}. For (b)$\subseteq$(c):
by Dung's fundamental lemma, if $S$ is admissible and $S$ defends $a$ then
$S\cup\{a\}$ is admissible; a $\subseteq$-maximal admissible $S$ therefore
already contains every argument it defends, i.e.\ it is complete. Any complete
$T\supseteq S$ is in particular admissible, so $T=S$ by maximality of $S$
among admissible sets; hence $S$ is maximal complete. For
(c)$\subseteq$(b): let $S$ be $\subseteq$-maximal complete. $S$ is admissible;
by finiteness extend it to a $\subseteq$-maximal admissible $T\supseteq S$.
By the previous part $T$ is complete, so maximality of $S$ among complete
extensions forces $S=T$, and $S$ is maximal admissible.
See also the labelling-based account of \citet{caminada2009}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Preferred construction]
\label{thm:prf}
For every finite AAF (encoded as in Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}), the
$\pred{in}$-projections of
$\ASstar_{\pred{in}}(P_{\mathrm{adm}}\cup\Daaf)$ --- equivalently of
$\ASstar_{\pred{in}}(P_{\mathrm{cmp}}\cup\Daaf)$ --- are exactly the preferred
extensions.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}, the $\pred{in}$-projections of the answer sets of
$P_{\mathrm{adm}}\cup\Daaf$ are exactly the admissible sets, and those of
$P_{\mathrm{cmp}}\cup\Daaf$ exactly the complete extensions; moreover distinct
answer sets have distinct $\pred{in}$-projections, so subset-maximality of an
answer set's $\pred{in}$-projection is subset-maximality within the family of
admissible (respectively complete) sets. By
Proposition~\ref{prop:prfmax}, the $\subseteq$-maximal members of either
family are exactly the preferred extensions.
\end{proof}

The learning pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:exp1} implements this
construction: the learner induces an admissible or complete core from
preferred-labelled examples, and the evaluation applies the same fixed
subset-maximal $\pred{in}$-selection \emph{symmetrically} to the learned and
the reference side. Theorem~\ref{thm:prf} covers the canonical cores of
Figure~\ref{fig:learned}; campaign-learned cores are typically
constraint-style presentations over the guessing background
(Section~\ref{sec:exp1surfaces}), whose behaviour the campaign's
complete-information test checks per run. This is what makes preferred behave
like the other three semantics in the campaign --- the maximality step is
fixed by the construction, so the learnable content is a tight core.

\subsection{Case study: a defect in the published ABA translation}
\label{sec:abadefect}

The argument-enumeration step of the published ABA-to-AAF translation
constructs candidate arguments in a \emph{single} enumeration pass that
couples the choice of assumption set with the choice of a derived root, and
discards candidates dominated in a subset comparison over the coupled
assumption-and-root vocabulary. This is incorrect: domination in the coupled
vocabulary is not domination among the supports of any one claim, so required
arguments are discarded. Two failure modes occur:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Minimal counterexample}: $\mathcal R=\{s_1\leftarrow\}$,
$\mathcal A=\{s_0\}$. The assumption argument $(\,s_0,\{s_0\}\,)$ is required
by the definition but never enumerated: the assumption-free argument
$(\,s_1,\emptyset\,)$ dominates --- in the coupled comparison --- every
candidate that assumes anything, so all of them are discarded.
\item Even with no assumption-free derivation anywhere, candidates sharing a
subset-minimal assumption set but differing in claim can block one another
through the coupled comparison. With $\mathcal R=\{s_5\leftarrow s_1\}$,
$\mathcal A=\{s_1\}$, the published program returns only $(\,s_1,\{s_1\}\,)$
and drops the required argument $(\,s_5,\{s_1\}\,)$.
\item The failure is not rare: on 500 random flat ABA frameworks, the
published program drops required arguments in \textbf{378 cases} ($76\%$) ---
$296$ involving the first mode, $82$ the second alone.
\item The published running example is benign: it happens to produce no
dominated candidates, which is why the defect is invisible there.
Correctness had been argued by example.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The corrected construction (Section~\ref{sec:abatranslation})
decouples the two choices --- one subset-minimal enumeration over the
assumption vocabulary alone, per pinned root --- and is correct by
Proposition~\ref{prop:aba}; it also agrees with an independently written
reference enumeration on all 500 random frameworks. Downstream, the defect
matters: missing AAF arguments silently
change extensions, hence any learned or computed semantics of the translated
framework. We draw the obvious methodological moral: encodings whose
correctness is argued by example --- \emph{a fortiori} learned encodings ---
warrant proof. The corrected translation is used throughout
Sections~\ref{sec:exp1}--\ref{sec:exp2}.

\section{Experiment 1: Learnability under Imperfect Data}
\label{sec:exp1}

The published evaluations of semantics learning --- ours included --- use small,
manually curated example sets with clean labels. Any application to real data
(Section~\ref{sec:exp2}) instead produces examples that are \emph{sampled}
(from whatever frameworks occur), \emph{incomplete} (not every argument gets
labelled), \emph{noisy} (labellers err), and \emph{imbalanced} (positives and
negatives are not matched). Experiment~1 maps semantics \emph{recovery} across
these four axes in a single controlled campaign: $3{,}240$ learning runs,
grouped $5$-fold cross-validation, four semantics.\footnote{The released
artifact additionally contains $270$ grounded-semantics runs from the same
campaign infrastructure; they are excluded from all analyses per the scoping
footnote of Section~\ref{sec:bg-af}. Grounded is studied separately, and
recovered exactly, in \ref{app:grd}.}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp1setup}

\paragraph{Frameworks and labels.} We generate 500 random AAFs (100 per size
$n\in\{4,\dots,8\}$; attack count uniform in $[n,\,n(n-1)]$, i.e.\ a dense
regime; uniqueness enforced) and label pools with the ASPARTIX reference
encodings: positives are $\sigma$-extensions rendered as
$\pred{in}/\pred{out}$ labellings; negatives are oracle-verified
non-extensions. At label completeness $p<1$, each label atom of a positive
example is retained independently with probability $p$ (the CDPI semantics
makes partial examples natively meaningful). At noise rate $q$, an example's
\emph{polarity is inverted} with probability $q$ --- a correct labelling
presented as negative, or a verified non-extension presented as positive ---
and all examples in noisy cells carry finite penalties, so the learner may
discount them at a cost.

\paragraph{Grid.} Semantics $\{$STB, ADM, CMP, PRF$\}$ $\times$
$p\in\{1.0,0.75,0.5\}$ $\times$ $q\in\{0,0.1,0.2\}$ $\times$ training-set
sizes of $f\in\{10,20,30,40,60,80\}$ examples per class at three positive
proportions ($40\%$, $50\%$, $60\%$ of a fixed total, so imbalance is isolated
from sample size) $\times$ $5$ folds $=3{,}240$ runs. Folds are cut at the
level of \emph{source frameworks} (grouped cross-validation), so no framework
contributes to both training and test.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} The learned program is scored on $100$ held-out
positive and $100$ held-out negative labelled instances from unseen frameworks
by exact-model matching: a positive instance counts as correctly classified iff
its labelling is an answer set of the learned program on that framework, a
negative iff it is not. Negatives are scored on two surfaces. The
\emph{sampled} surface uses the pool's oracle-verified non-extensions; the
\emph{adversarial} surface replaces them, for the same $100$ held-out
positives, with oracle-verified \emph{Hamming-1} negatives --- a single
$\pred{in}/\pred{out}$ label of a held-out true extension is flipped and the
result kept only if the reference encoding rejects it, so every adversarial
negative is a genuine non-extension one flip away from a genuine extension
(the hardest labelling-level negatives that exist). The filter itself
measures how sharp each semantics is at Hamming distance~$1$: no single flip
of a stable or preferred extension is ever an extension, whereas $32.2\%$ of
flips of admissible and $17.7\%$ of complete labellings remain legal and are
discarded. Headline numbers use the sampled surface;
Table~\ref{tab:hardsurface} reports the adversarial one. We report Matthews
correlation (MCC). Crucially, the
test surface is \emph{complete-information}: even when training labels are
partial, test instances carry full labellings --- otherwise recovery is
systematically understated (a learner that recovers the exact semantics can
still ``fail'' a partial test instance whose missing labels admit multiple
completions). Training failures are recorded in a first-class taxonomy ---
timeout ($3{,}500$\,s cap), unsatisfiable, error --- rather than folded into
scores.

\paragraph{Preferred at scale.} PRF cells instantiate the construction of
Theorem~\ref{thm:prf}: the learner induces an admissible/complete core from
the ordinary guessing background (nothing maximality-related appears in the
learning task), and the subset-maximal $\pred{in}$-selection $\ASstar_{\pred{in}}$
is applied at \emph{evaluation} time, symmetrically to the learned and the
reference side. Training pools include frameworks whose preferred and stable
extensions differ, so a stable ``shortcut'' theory cannot survive the
negatives. The historically reported failures of preferred at scale trace to
formulation, not to the semantics: asking the learner to induce the maximality
step itself, and omitting the subset-maximal selection on the evaluation side;
with the formulation of Theorem~\ref{thm:prf} preferred behaves like the other
three semantics throughout.

data keeps buying semantic correctness after MCC stops registering it.

sample-test caveat concrete --- behaviourally indistinguishable from the

exact on every framework with up to four arguments yet score below $1.0$ on
test frameworks with five to eight arguments. The audit lab is
\texttt{analysis/exp1\_exactness\_audit/}.

\textbf{(E1.2) Noise is the binding constraint --- amplified by incompleteness,
bought back by data.} Label noise, not sample size or partiality, is what
degrades recovery: at $q=0.2$, small-sample recovery drops to $0.48$--$0.74$,
but every semantics climbs back to $0.94$--$0.96$ by $f=80$ (on completed
runs; see the survivorship accounting below). One compositional caveat makes
this claim sharper, not weaker: at $q=0.2$, $f=80$ the half-complete stratum
almost never finishes ($2/20$ runs at $p=0.5$), so the pooled $f=80$ cell of
Table~\ref{tab:surface} is effectively a $p\ge0.75$ mean. Stratifying
confirms the buy-back where completion is total: at $p\ge0.75$ all $40/40$
runs complete and recovery at $f=80$ is $0.938$ ($p{=}1.0$) and $0.957$
($p{=}0.75$), i.e.\ $0.94$--$0.96$ per semantics with \emph{no} survivorship
conditioning; at $p=0.5$ the honest statement is the joint accuracy--compute
claim of Section~\ref{sec:exp1cost}, not a recovery number. The completeness axis
(Table~\ref{tab:pq}) sharpens this: partial labels cost \emph{nothing} on
clean data --- if anything they help, since partial positives constrain less
and are easier to cover --- but they multiply the damage of noise
($0.872\to0.619$ at $q=0.2$ as $p$ falls from $1.0$ to $0.5$). Figures
\ref{fig:slice_noise} and~\ref{fig:slice_partial} make the interaction concrete
at a single sample size ($f=20$, where all cells are complete): under
\emph{full} labels the noise response is gentle and its fold-to-fold
variance small, whereas under \emph{half} labels the same noise both lowers
recovery and inflates its variance sharply (Figure~\ref{fig:slice_noise}); and
sweeping completeness directly (Figure~\ref{fig:slice_partial}) shows the lines
essentially flat on clean data but fanning downward once noise is present.
Recovery is \emph{not} saturated by sample size alone at imperfect information
--- the $f$-axis keeps paying off precisely when labels are partial or noisy, a
correction to the ``saturation at $f\ge 30$'' reading a clean-label study
would suggest.

\subsection{Cost and failure frontier}
\label{sec:exp1cost}

Of $3{,}240$ runs, $3{,}120$ completed, $120$ timed out at the $3{,}500$\,s
cap, and \emph{none} ended unsatisfiable or in error. The failure mass is
sharply localized: zero timeouts at $q=0$, $18$ at $q=0.1$, $102$ at $q=0.2$,
concentrated at large $f$ and low $p$ --- the same corner where median training
time rises from seconds (clean) to hundreds of seconds (noisy;
Figure~\ref{fig:time_heatmap}): learning under
noise is an optimization over exception sets, and its cost, like its accuracy
loss, is driven by the noise axis. The exclusions are material where E1.2's
high-noise recovery claim lives: on the balanced arm at $q=0.2$, $55/60$ runs
completed at $f=40$, $45/60$ at $f=60$, and $42/60$ at $f=80$. The reported
means are therefore conditional on completion; under the worst-case sensitivity
of scoring every timed-out run as MCC $0$, the $q=0.2$ pooled means would fall
from $0.937$ to $0.703$ ($f=60$) and from $0.950$ to $0.665$ ($f=80$). The
honest reading is a joint accuracy--compute claim: \emph{within the compute
budget, noise costs either accuracy or completions}; the taxonomy makes the
trade explicit instead of hiding it.

\subsection{Breadth: other generators, other frameworks}
\label{sec:exp1breadth}

Three follow-up experiments test whether Experiment~1's conclusions are
artifacts of its generator regime or of the AAF setting.

\paragraph{Generator breadth.} We re-ran an anchor slice of the grid
($p\in\{1.0,0.5\}$, $q\in\{0,0.1\}$, $f\in\{20,60\}$, five folds) on three
structurally different pools: \emph{sparse} frameworks (attack count uniform in
$[n,2n]$ --- the regime of most benchmark and human-scale graphs),
\emph{self-attacking} frameworks (self-attacks permitted), and \emph{larger}
frameworks ($n\in\{10,11,12\}$). Figure~\ref{fig:generator_breadth} compares
recovery at $f=60$ against the dense pool; the finding is not a flat ``it
reproduces everywhere'' but a sharper, regime-dependent one.

\emph{Clean labels reproduce across all three regimes.} At $q=0$ every
generator recovers every semantics at MCC $\ge 0.99$ for stable, admissible,
and complete, and $\ge 0.97$ for preferred (bar one exception below) --- the
learnability of the semantics does not depend on the generator, and in
particular it \emph{scales to $n=12$}, where clean-label recovery is $0.99$--
$1.00$ throughout. This is the core robustness claim, and it holds cleanly.

\emph{Under noise the regimes separate.} Sparse tracks the dense pool closely
(within $\pm0.03$ at matched coordinates, and \emph{better} for complete, $0.94$
vs.\ $0.69$ at $f{=}20$ --- sparser graphs are if anything easier). Larger
frameworks confirm scaling but with heavier censoring: $25$ of their $160$ runs
hit the $3{,}500$\,s cap (versus $1$ for sparse), so the surviving noisy-large
numbers are survivor-biased upward and we read them as suggestive, not
definitive. The same worst-case sensitivity as Section~\ref{sec:exp1cost}
(scoring every timed-out run as MCC~$0$) bounds the damage: most noisy-large
cells drop from $0.87$--$0.99$ to $0.61$--$0.89$, with the worst cell (PRF,
$q{=}0.1$, $f{=}60$: $5/10$ completions) falling from $0.945$ to $0.472$ ---
the truth lies between the conditional mean and this floor. The one genuine boundary is \emph{preferred on self-attacking
frameworks}: PRF recovery falls to $0.71$ at $q{=}0.1,f{=}60$ (and $0.42$ at
$f{=}20$), well below the $0.94$--$0.97$ the other regimes hold, and it is
depressed even at clean labels ($0.87$ vs.\ $0.98$). Self-attacks complicate
conflict-freeness and subset-maximality --- a self-attacking argument is in no
admissible set --- so the maximality-based construction is the most fragile
under label noise. We report this as a documented limitation of the current
formulation, not a gap: it is a specific, mechanistically explained boundary,
and the other three semantics remain robust across all three generators.

\paragraph{Cross-pool transfer.} The breadth re-runs above retrain on each
pool; a sharper question is whether a program learned on \emph{one} generator
classifies labellings from \emph{another}. Because the target semantics is
topology-invariant, evaluation-only transfer is the sharpest available test:
for an exactly recovered presentation, cross-pool MCC $1.0$ is entailed by
Theorems~\ref{thm:aaf} and~\ref{thm:prf}, so transfer measures how far the
campaign's \emph{actual} hypotheses sit from that guarantee off-distribution.
We re-scored every successful anchor-slice program ($595$ of $640$; the $45$
training timeouts remain excluded, never scored as zeros) --- with no
retraining --- on fixed complete-information surfaces of $80{+}80$
oracle-labelled items drawn from the \emph{other} pools' committed frameworks
(dense${}\to{}$\{sparse, self, large\} and each back to dense; $913$
cross-pool evaluations; the scorer reproduces the archived per-fold confusion
counts exactly on all $16$ validation rows). Across density and size shifts --- the $612$ non-self evaluations; the
self-attacking pool is treated separately below --- transfer is essentially
lossless (Table~\ref{tab:transfer}): clean-cell
programs average MCC $0.979$ cross-pool against $0.983$ within-pool; programs
with a \emph{perfect} within-pool score transfer at mean $0.994$ (exactly
$1.0$ in $82\%$ of these evaluations); and within-pool MCC predicts
cross-pool MCC at $r=0.91$, with $80\%$ of evaluations within $\pm 0.05$ ---
noisy-cell hypotheses likewise transfer at approximately their within-pool
score ($0.897$ vs.\ $0.909$ at $q{=}0.1$). The self-attacking pool is again the
boundary, and transfer sharpens its diagnosis: both directions lose about
$0.05$ MCC even at clean labels, and the worst case is instructive --- one
clean dense-learned complete hypothesis contains the constraint
\texttt{:- att(V1,V1).}, vacuous on the dense pool (which contains no
self-attacks; within-pool MCC $0.96$) but rejecting every self-attacking
positive (transfer MCC $0.0$). Within-pool cross-validation cannot detect a
hypothesis that has absorbed a generator invariant; evaluation-only transfer
is precisely the audit that does. The record is committed under
\texttt{data/transfer/}.

\section{From Synthetic Recovery to Human Judgements: the Bridge}
\label{sec:bridge}

Experiment~1 is not only a robustness study; it is the \emph{calibration
instrument} that makes Experiment~2's conclusions safe. Four specific licenses
carry over.

\textbf{(B1) The pipeline is not the bottleneck --- and the gate establishes it in
Experiment 2's own currency.} We operationalize pipeline soundness as a hard
\emph{synthetic gate}: every behavioural pipeline variant must first recover
clean \emph{textbook} labels end-to-end on the very graphs of the human study,
scored with Experiment~2's own metric, before any human-data result from it is
reported. On the grounded-semantics gate, all three pipeline variants (ILASP
at rule arity one, FastLAS OPL, FastLAS NOPL) score $1.000$ on both the
three-valued gate metric (pass threshold $0.95$) and committed-only accuracy
($n=495$) --- a constraint theory can coincide with grounded extensionally on
these few small study graphs even though grounded is not
constraint-expressible in general (footnote of Section~\ref{sec:bg-af}); the
gate tests the pipeline on these graphs, not general expressibility. The gate also \emph{caught} a real limitation: on the
stable-semantics gate, ILASP's arity-one setting reaches only $0.921$ on the
gate metric (fail) and $0.882$ committed-only --- an expressivity ceiling of
that restricted
configuration on these graphs, which the ILASP rows of
Section~\ref{sec:exp2} therefore inherit as an explicit caveat. We also gate
CF2 itself --- the pre-registered comparator, and a multi-extension semantics,
so each distinct CF2 labelling of a study graph enters as its own clean cell.
FastLAS passes the credulous reading (the one behind committed-only accuracy)
with the primitive vocabulary at $0.971$; on the skeptical reading the
primitive vocabulary falls just short ($0.927$) and the SCC-cycle vocabulary
of Section~\ref{sec:aux} crosses the threshold ($0.956$) --- so CF2 \emph{is}
expressible in the hypothesis space on these graphs, with the skeptical-reading
learner rows carrying a mild primitive-vocabulary caveat that the
vocabulary-fair comparison of Table~\ref{tab:aux} discharges. ILASP's
arity-one setting fails the CF2 gate outright ($0.423$ skeptical), extending
its stable-gate caveat. This is the
metric-level license: when a \emph{gated} learner trained on human labellings
falls short of a catalogue semantics, the shortfall --- on the gated readings
--- cannot be attributed to the
learning machinery. Experiment~1's surface adds the sampled-data envelope
behind it: at behavioural sample sizes and clean labels, recovery is
$\ge0.92$ MCC (Table~\ref{tab:surface}, $q=0$).

\textbf{(B2) Oracle-free negatives are principled --- and quantified.}
Synthetic learning enjoys an oracle that verifies negatives; human data has
none. A dedicated calibration at Experiment~2's operating scale ($f{=}30$
examples per class, $3$ grouped folds, pooled over STB/ADM/CMP;
Table~\ref{tab:negcal}, per-fold record committed with the repository)
injects per-argument label-flip noise into training positives and compares
negative-generation policies by recovery against a clean oracle. On clean
positives the oracle-free policies sit $0.06$--$0.09$ MCC below the oracle
mean with overlapping fold ranges (reliable-negative $0.911$, Hamming-1
flip-one $0.881$, oracle $0.970$). Under noise the two oracle-free policies
separate exactly where theory predicts: flip-one matches reliable-negative up
to $q{=}0.1$ ($0.816$ vs $0.802$) but collapses at $q{=}0.2$ ($0.574$, one
fold below zero) while the density-based reliable-negative policy tracks the
oracle ceiling best ($0.742$ vs oracle $0.826$). The wrong-negative-rate
column exposes the mechanism: single-flip manufactures ``negatives'' that are
in fact legal labellings in $14$--$22\%$ of cases --- which \emph{soft}
penalties out-vote at low noise, but which compound with label noise ---
whereas reliable-negative stays at $\le3\%$. Experiment~2 accordingly pairs
Hamming-1 shells with soft penalties --- so a wrong near-miss negative can be
out-voted rather than enforced --- and balances negative against positive
penalty mass (Section~\ref{sec:exp2setup}).

\textbf{(B3) Error decomposes --- with the interaction read the right way.}
Reading Experiment~1's surface at coordinates matching the behavioural data
yields the \emph{recovery limit}: the best any learner could do if humans
followed some Dung-style semantics exactly but reported it imperfectly. The gap
between that limit and observed human-data accuracy is then attributable to
\emph{model mismatch} --- the extent to which human acceptance is not any such
semantics. Table~\ref{tab:pq} supplies the needed calibration facts:
incompleteness alone (humans abstaining) is essentially free for the learner,
noise alone (humans erring) is survivable and data-recoverable --- but their
\emph{combination} is what collapses recovery. Since the human corpus is both
small and heterogeneous, the decomposition warns that the learner will sit at
its recovery limit long before it exhausts the signal --- anticipating the
finding that the residual gap to CF2 is individual variation rather than a
missing structural principle (Section~\ref{sec:humansem}).

\textbf{(B4) The wide search is affordable.} ILASP's expressivity makes wide
hypothesis spaces expensive on noisy behavioural tasks ($74$--$135$\,s per
call in our setting). A reformulation insight --- learn a
\emph{verifier} of a labelling given as input, rather than the labelling as a
recursive target --- makes the task representable for FastLAS, which learns the
same theories $30$--$600\times$ faster ($0.2$--$0.3$\,s), byte-identical on
clean data (Section~\ref{sec:eval}). This is what makes the enriched feature
vocabulary and the multi-seed, multi-condition protocol of Experiment~2
computationally routine.

\section{Experiment 2: Learning the Semantics Humans Use}
\label{sec:exp2}

\citet{guillaume2022} established that, among textbook semantics, \emph{CF2}
best predicts how people accept and reject arguments. The result is
\emph{descriptive}: it selects the closest member of a fixed catalogue but does
not say what acceptance principles humans apply, nor whether those principles
can be \emph{learned} from behaviour. We revisit their data with the calibrated
machinery of Sections~\ref{sec:exp1}--\ref{sec:bridge}.

\subsection{Data, protocol, audit}
\label{sec:exp2setup}

We use the accept/reject/undecided judgements of the \citet{guillaume2022}
participants across seven conditions A--G: conditions A/B/C present a
four-argument \emph{floating-reinstatement} graph, D/E/F a three-argument
acyclic graph, and G a five-argument odd cycle; conditions sharing a structure
differ in argument \emph{content}. The study recruited $130$ participants; the
individual-graph pool analysed here comprises $129$ participants' final
drawings (one condition-G participant has none), each carrying a three-valued
labelling --- the study's $495$ argument-level responses.

Each labelling enters learning as a \emph{soft} positive example, pinned by a
soft negative shell: its Hamming-1 perturbations plus one all-undecided
labelling per graph (so that totality is representable). Penalty mass is
balanced so no correct semantics can be \emph{forbidden} by an example --- only
out-voted (Section~\ref{sec:bg-las}). Prediction is generate-and-test: guess a
labelling, keep those the learned constraints admit, project credulously to a
verdict per argument.

\paragraph{Metrics.} The primary metric is \emph{committed-only accuracy}: on
responses where a human took a stance (\pred{in}/\pred{out}), the fraction the
model predicts exactly, an undecided prediction counting as wrong. A uniform
coin scores $50\%$ and a three-way guesser $33.3\%$; the empirical
majority-class baseline (predict \pred{in} everywhere) scores $0.545$ on the
committed pool ($169$ \pred{in} vs $141$ \pred{out} responses). We also report
three-valued exact match under the skeptical reading of \citet{guillaume2022}
(chance $33.3\%$). Significance: exact two-sided McNemar tests against the
pre-registered comparator CF2, deduplicated to one unit per (graph, labelling,
argument) cell --- the estimand is thus \emph{distinct response patterns}, not
participant-weighted behaviour, which removes within-cell response
multiplicity. \emph{Test families.} We declare three confirmatory families
and apply Holm step-down within each: (F1) the learner-versus-CF2 deficit
tests of Section~\ref{sec:act1} (the two skeptical cell-level tests, one per
learner, plus FastLAS's committed cell-level and committed response-level
tests; $m=4$); (F2) the seven
direction-gated \textsc{within}${}>{}$\textsc{global} contextuality tests of
Section~\ref{sec:act3} ($m=7$); (F3) the two pooled
follow-up characterizations of Section~\ref{sec:act3} (the
\textsc{global}${}>{}$\textsc{within} reversal and the
\textsc{within}-versus-CF2 comparison, $m=2$). F2 tests the directional
hypothesis \textsc{within}${}>{}$\textsc{global} and F3 the opposite
direction after F2's rejection, so the two cannot share a directional family.

\paragraph{Audit.} Every reported number survives an adversarial audit:
cross-validation folds are cut at distinct (graph, labelling) cells so no
held-out response's exact training example can leak (including across
conditions D/E/F, which share graphs); all-undecided participants are retained,
reproducing the study's $495$-response pool exactly; each pipeline is gated on
clean textbook labels before any human result is trusted (B1: grounded,
stable, and CF2 gates --- including the
disclosed ILASP stable- and CF2-gate shortfalls); and our textbook
baselines reproduce the published individual-graph accuracy column to within
$1.2$ points. We flag this prominently because our own first pass produced an
\emph{inflated} learner-vs-catalogue gap through three pipeline artifacts
(hard-negative contamination, choice-rule prediction inflation, and a deflated
macro-average) and an unreachable gold standard; all were found by the audit,
fixed, and the analysis re-run --- the direction of every conclusion below
survived, with honest magnitudes.

\subsection{The contextual-reasoning test: one semantics, not seven}
\label{sec:act3}

\section{The Semantics People Follow}
\label{sec:humansem}

Section~\ref{sec:act1} says the learner does not beat CF2; it does not say the
learner is uninformative. Extracting the rules that recur across
cross-validation folds (discarding per-participant noise) yields the compact,
fold-stable acceptance theory of Figure~\ref{fig:humansem} --- learned
agnostically, from behaviour alone. Its scope is the evidence that induced
it: the pooled judgements of these $129$ participants on these seven graph
conditions.

\subsection{The learned theory}
\label{sec:theory}

The theory is a labelling \emph{verifier} (Section~\ref{sec:bridge}, B4): a
labelling is admissible-to-a-human unless a rule fires.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\small
\fbox{\parbox{0.93\linewidth}{

\item[Justification] accept nothing that is neither defended nor attacked by

\end{description}}}
\caption{The human semantics $\sigma_H$ as learned. Rules 1--3 are
near-textbook --- jointly, a \emph{committal} complete-like policy with cycle
handling, though rule 1's escape clause tolerates conflict among accepted
arguments (see the principle profile below). Rule 4 is a
\emph{candidate} hedge: psychologically suggestive --- graded and
ranking-based semantics posit exactly this attack-load sensitivity
axiomatically~\citep{grossi2019graded,amgoud2013ranking,bonzon2016ranking}
--- but it recurs in only $5$ of $20$ cross-seed folds and its antecedent is
realized exactly once in the corpus (see text).}
\label{fig:humansem}
\end{figure}

Three observations locate $\sigma_H$ against the catalogue. \emph{(i)} On
acyclic graphs rules 1--2 forbid fence-sitting --- humans do not sit on the
fence when the logic is unambiguous, which is why undecided-heavy semantics
like grounded fit poorly on the simple conditions --- and the unique complete
($=$ grounded $=$ preferred $=$ stable) labelling is legal on $536$ of the
$572$ acyclic AFs with $n\le4$ (the $36$ exceptions are rule-4 totality gaps;
see the principle profile below). But $\sigma_H$ does not \emph{coincide}
with forced commitment: its legal labellings strictly include the complete
one on $532$ of those $536$ --- most visibly, rule 1's exception also admits
$\{a,b\}$ on $a\!\to\!b$, tolerating an accepted argument already ``handled''
by an accepted attacker.
\emph{(ii)} On cycles, rule 3 withholds the commitment bias --- qualitatively
the direction CF2 formalizes. \emph{(iii)} Rule 4, if genuine, would make
$\sigma_H$ \emph{depart most sharply from the catalogue}: an argument whose
attackers are all defeated would remain tainted if the attackers were many. This
violates the reinstatement principle that all extension-based Dung-style
semantics share~\citep{dung1995,baroni2011} and echoes the graded weakening of
reinstatement observed behaviourally by \citet{rahwan2010}. Attack-load
sensitivity is not new as a \emph{formal} principle: graded
acceptability~\citep{grossi2019graded} parameterizes Dung's defense by the
number of attackers and defenders precisely to weaken reinstatement, and
cardinality precedence --- more attackers is worse --- is a core axiom of the
ranking-based semantics programme~\citep{amgoud2013ranking,bonzon2016ranking}.
What is distinctive here is the epistemic route: the hedge \emph{surfaces}
from judgement data as an explicit, checkable rule inside a single learned
policy, rather than being posited axiomatically. Precisely because it is
checkable, we stress-tested it --- and demote it to a candidate.

Two direct checks temper rule 4, and sharpen rules 1--3. \emph{Stability}:
re-extracting the consensus at four independent fold seeds ($4\times5=20$
learned theories per vocabulary) leaves the core untouched --- justification
recurs in $20/20$ folds, commitment in $16/20$ (its audited accept-defended
half in $10/20$; a weaker defended-not-rejected variant in $13/20$), cycle
caution in $15/20$ --- but the exact smoke/fire rule recurs
in only $5/20$, and at one seed it is absent altogether (only an
acyclic-restricted variant survives there, in $2$ of $5$ folds).
\emph{Behaviour}: computing the rule's two predicates directly on all $495$
(participant, argument) units, a fully reinstated argument with two or more
attackers occurs exactly \emph{once} in the corpus --- and that participant
accepted it. The graded contrast the rule asserts is therefore untestable on
these stimuli (single-attacker reinstated units are accepted at $63/95$;
under the behavioural reading of defeat --- all attackers labelled
\pred{out} --- the many-attacker cell again holds one unit). Where, then,
does the learner's evidence live? On the synthetic side of the task: the rule
fires on $1$ of $129$ human labellings but on $11$ of $262$ Hamming-1
negatives. Rule 4 is thus a learner-surfaced \emph{hypothesis} consistent
with graded reinstatement~\citep{rahwan2010,grossi2019graded} --- an
extrapolation beyond the support of the data, not an established behavioural
regularity; the zero-shot predictions below are exactly the experiment that
would decide it.

\paragraph{Principle profile of $\sigma_H$.}
Because $\sigma_H$ is an executable verifier, its position in the
principle-based catalogue of \citet{baroni2011} need not be conjectured: we
audited it on all $66{,}066$ labelled digraphs with $n\le4$ arguments
(self-attacks included), computing per framework the full set of labellings
$\sigma_H$ admits ($1{,}061{,}584$ in total) and testing each principle
mechanically (\texttt{analysis/sigma\_h\_principles/}).
Table~\ref{tab:sigmah} gives the profile. Two relations sharpen it. First,
$\sigma_H$ is \emph{permissive} rather than deviant: it almost never
coincides with the complete labellings, but it \emph{contains} them on most
frameworks --- every complete labelling is admitted on $77.7\%$ of the
$66{,}066$, on $536$ of the $572$ acyclic frameworks, and on all the stimulus
graph shapes of the experiment, where $\sigma_H$ is also total. Second, the two
headline failures are exactly attributable: re-running the audit with rule 4
deleted restores totality on all $66{,}066$ frameworks, and re-running it
with rule 1's exception removed (``accept nothing that is not defended'',
with no attacked-by-accepted escape) restores conflict-freeness on all of
them (admissibility's witness coincides with conflict-freeness's; the
reinstatement and directionality failures are separate and our ablations do
not attribute them to a single rule).

We emphasize the epistemic status of $\sigma_H$: it is a fold-stable
\emph{description} of the pooled behaviour of $129$ participants on seven
graphs --- a learned consensus policy, not a proposal for a new normative
semantics; its cycle rule stabilizes in $15$ of $20$ cross-seed folds, while
the smoke/fire hedge recurs in only $5$ of $20$ and is offered as a
falsifiable candidate, not a finding. Its value
is precisely that it is \emph{legible}: each rule can be inspected, tested on
new graphs, and traced back to the data that induced it. The principle
profile is exactly why we offer $\sigma_H$ as a description and not a
proposal: a policy that tolerates conflict among accepted arguments (rule
1's exception) and refuses forced reinstatement to the point of leaving some
frameworks with no admitted labelling at all (rule 4) departs from
\emph{every} Dung-style catalogue semantics on independent, mechanically
checkable axes --- which sharpens, rather than weakens, the claim that what
people do is not a known semantics. Legibility also
makes $\sigma_H$ falsifiable in print. We freeze two zero-shot predictions
for external stimuli that were not used anywhere in this paper: on
single-attacker reinstatement chains of the kind studied by
\citet{rahwan2010}, rule 4 does not fire, so $\sigma_H$ predicts full,
committal reinstatement --- consistent with the reinstatement those authors
observe, though not with its graded weakening, which $\sigma_H$'s
attack-\emph{count} trigger cannot express; on any reinstatement graph whose
reinstated argument has two or more attackers, $\sigma_H$ predicts
\emph{blocked} reinstatement, diverging from every extension-based catalogue
semantics. A pre-registered test of these two predictions on new behavioural
data is the natural next step (Section~\ref{sec:concl}).

\subsection{Auxiliary predicates: what closes the gap, and what does not}
\label{sec:aux}

Rule 4 references vocabulary (\pred{reinstated}, \pred{many\_attackers}) that
the primitive $\pred{arg}/\pred{att}$ language cannot state in a single
low-arity rule. We therefore searched four families of psychologically
motivated \emph{auxiliary predicates}. Two help: \emph{degree/salience}
(\pred{unattacked}, \pred{many\_attackers}, \pred{has\_unattacked\_attacker})
and \emph{reinstatement/defense} (\pred{reinstated}, \pred{floating},
\pred{undefended\_in}). Two do not --- most pointedly, \emph{SCC/cycle}
predicates, the natural vocabulary of CF2, which the learner simply ignored.
Adding the two useful families lifts \emph{in-corpus} committed-only
accuracy (five-fold CV on the full pooled corpus) from $0.571$ to
$0.648$ on average --- $+7.7$ points, positive at every one of four independent
fold seeds (range $+5.5$ to $+9.7$; an exact sign test on $n=4$ seeds gives
$p=.0625$, the minimum attainable, so the multi-seed table is offered as a
robustness check rather than an inferential claim) --- closing roughly a third
of the gap to CF2 (Table~\ref{tab:aux}).

\paragraph{Selection honesty.} Because the two families were chosen on the
same corpus Table~\ref{tab:aux} scores, we re-ran the entire selection
pipeline with each condition held out (leave-one-condition-out,
identity-guarded as in the transfer arm of Section~\ref{sec:act3}; canonical
seed; the selection rule codified as: a family is kept iff its
cross-validated committed-only accuracy strictly beats the primitive

ranked by learned-theory frequency under the same five-predicate cap).
Family-level selection is only partly stable: reinstatement/defense is
re-selected in $6$ of $7$ folds and degree/salience in $5$, but
reach-position --- dropped as neutral on the full corpus --- crosses its
baseline in $6$ of $7$, and the SCC/cycle family crosses in $2$ (by at most
$+1.3$ points; its predicates never survive the cap). And the gain does not
transfer: scoring each fold's selection-honest vocabulary on its held-out
condition against a primitive-vocabulary theory trained on the identical
six-condition set gives per-condition deltas from $-57.5$ to $+29.6$ points
(median $0.0$), pooled $+2.6$ points ($0.581$ vs.\ $0.555$ committed-only,

\emph{in-corpus} gain: the auxiliary features reliably help \emph{describe}
the seven conditions they were selected on, but a vocabulary chosen without
seeing a condition does not reliably help \emph{predict} it --- consistent
with Section~\ref{sec:act3}'s finding that what transfers across conditions
is the policy, with the vocabulary's value concentrated in-condition
(record: \texttt{results/aux10\_loco.json}).


The identity of the winning features suggests a hypothesis the catalogue
comparison could not deliver: that CF2's remaining edge over the learner is
\emph{not} cycle bookkeeping --- the CF2 gate (B1) shows the SCC family
demonstrably suffices to express CF2 on these graphs (gate $0.956$ on the
skeptical reading), yet on human data the learner makes at best marginal use
of those predicates (in the leave-one-condition-out re-selection the SCC
family crosses its baseline in only $2$ of $7$ folds, by at most $+1.3$
points, and never enters the capped vocabulary); the edge would instead be a
residue of individual variation that salience and floating-reinstatement
features only partly capture. We state this as a hypothesis rather than a
finding because the same check shows family selection is only partially
stable at this corpus size. Combined with the contextual test of Section~\ref{sec:act3},
the picture is: one structure-sensitive policy, keyed on salience and
reinstatement features, with an irreducible individual-level noise floor ---
exactly the decomposition (B3) anticipated: the learner is at its recovery
limit; what remains is model mismatch between \emph{any} single semantics and a
heterogeneous population.

\section{Comparative Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

We close the empirical loop with the two comparisons the field most often asks
for. Both are restated from the preliminary evaluation of this
framework\footnote{arXiv:2310.12309; PAR-2 timings on a MacBook Pro M1
(2020), computing one extension per instance. The comparisons were not re-run
for this paper; we restate them with the preliminary report's own numbers,
correcting one overclaim.} rather than re-run; the correction below is ours.

\paragraph{Time performance of learned encodings.} On the ICCMA'23 benchmark
set~\citep{iccma23}, measuring PAR-2 for computing one extension: the learned
encodings are markedly faster than ASPARTIX for admissible and stable, at
parity for complete, and \emph{slower} for preferred --- where ASPARTIX's
specialized encoding outperforms our declarative subset-maximal selection.
We state this honestly, correcting the preliminary report's abstract-level
claim of outperforming existing solvers: learned encodings are competitive,
sometimes better, not uniformly dominant.

\paragraph{Data efficiency versus deep learning.} The approximate solver
AGNN~\citep{craandijkbex2020} reaches MCC $0.998$--$0.999$ for stable,
preferred, and complete after training on $10^{6}$ labelled frameworks. As
reported in the preliminary evaluation, the LAS route reaches MCC $=1.0$ ---
with a \emph{provably correct} theory --- from $7$ (admissible), $8$ (stable,
complete), and $16$ (preferred) examples on frameworks of at most five
arguments; conversely, capping AGNN's training set at $30$ examples collapses
it to MCC $\le 0.31$ on the semantics studied here (stable $0.054$, complete
$0.213$, preferred $0.303$). The comparison is not adversarial --- AGNN
targets amortized inference speed, not data efficiency --- but it quantifies
the regime where interpretable learning is the only viable option: when
labelled data is scarce, as in behavioural settings (Section~\ref{sec:exp2})
where the \emph{entire corpus} is $495$ responses.

\paragraph{ILP engines.} Within the LAS family: ILASP's full expressivity is
required for learning \emph{generative} encodings (choice-rule hypothesis
spaces, Section~\ref{sec:exp1}); FastLAS, under the verifier reformulation
(B4), learns constraint-style theories $30$--$600\times$ faster and identically
on clean data --- the workhorse of Experiment~2's wide-vocabulary protocol.
The reformulation is a transferable lesson: FastLAS's observational predicate
learning cannot use body features defined recursively from the learning
target; making the labelling an \emph{input} and learning its \emph{verifier}
removes the recursion without changing the semantics of the task.

\paragraph{A non-LAS baseline.} Both learners above are LAS-family; a fair
question is whether a simpler, different learner is competitive. The natural
comparator outside the family is a feature-based classifier on the
\emph{per-argument} acceptance task --- the task the deep-learning baselines
solve (predict whether an argument is credulously/skeptically accepted), rather
than the labelling-level extension-membership task the LAS encodings solve. We
train a decision tree, a $200$-tree random forest, and logistic regression on
$15$ purely structural argument features (degree, unattacked, in-a-cycle,
reachable-from-unattacked, SCC size, $\dots$), on the \emph{same} $500$-framework
pool under the \emph{same} grouped five-fold protocol, for all four semantics and
the same noise axis. Because credulous acceptance coincides for
admissible/complete/preferred, the per-semantics signal lives in the credulous
stable, skeptical stable/preferred/complete, and shared credulous targets.
Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_comparison} reports clean-label recovery. The
strongest feature learner per target (so this is not a strawman) tops out at
MCC $0.67$--$0.72$ on the multi-extension targets (the random forest) and
$0.86$ on complete/grounded (the depth-12 tree)\footnote{Skeptical-complete acceptance coincides with
grounded acceptance (the grounded extension is the least complete extension),
so grounded enters the per-argument baseline for free even though grounded
extension-learning is outside Experiment~1's scope (Section~\ref{sec:bg-af};
it is recovered exactly, with a definite background, in \ref{app:grd});
the LAS reference on this target is the complete encoding.} --- the
near-functional single-extension case where local features suffice ---
against the LAS route's exact clean-label recovery on these targets
(MCC $1.0$; Section~\ref{sec:exp1}). The interpretable depth-6
tree is lower ($0.55$--$0.82$). Under noise every feature learner loses
ground, but unevenly: on the grounded target the depth-12 tree collapses
($0.86\to0.45$ at $q{=}0.2$), the depth-6 tree drops $0.82\to0.65$, and even
the most noise-robust model, logistic regression, sits at $0.76$ --- and none
has a route back: more data does not restore the plateau, whereas the LAS
route buys recovery back to $0.94$--$0.96$ MCC under the same noise
(Section~\ref{sec:exp1}). Two
things separate the baseline from LAS: acceptance is a \emph{global} property
of the extension structure that local features can only approximate, and a
one-shot per-argument classifier structurally \emph{cannot represent the set
of extensions} at all (structured or autoregressive predictors could in
principle enumerate extension sets; none would be interpretable in the
rule-level sense sought here). We also considered single-intended-model ILP
systems (Popper, Aleph) as baselines. Noise is not what rules them out ---
Aleph tolerates noisy examples via its \texttt{noise}/\texttt{minacc}
settings~\citep{srinivasan2001aleph}, and recent Popper variants learn from
noisy data~\citep{hocquette2024maxsynth}. What rules them out is the learning
target: a hypothesis judged by which \emph{answer sets} it has, so that the
extensions are the answer sets themselves --- learning such programs requires
brave induction~\citep{sakama2009brave}, which the LAS framework strictly
generalizes~\citep{law2018phd}. The per-argument projection of the task
\emph{is} expressible in those systems, but that projection is exactly the
acceptance-classification task the feature baselines above already evaluate
with a strong learner (Section~\ref{sec:related}).

Inspecting the learned trees explains \emph{why} the gap has the shape it does
(Figure~\ref{fig:learned_trees}). For grounded acceptance the depth-6 tree
collapses --- after pruning branches that all predict the same class --- to three
legible rules: reject anything not reachable from an unattacked argument; reject
anything with an unattacked attacker; otherwise accept. This is a recognisable,
hand-readable approximation of grounded semantics, which is exactly why grounded
is the target the tree fits best. But it is a \emph{two-hop} approximation of a
\emph{fixpoint}: grounded acceptance follows arbitrarily long defence chains,
which no bounded-depth tree over local features can express, so the tree tops
out at MCC $0.82$ ($0.86$ for its depth-12 variant) rather than the LAS
program's exact $1.0$. For credulous-stable
acceptance the same top rule survives (an unattacked attacker forces rejection),
but below it the tree dissolves into roughly twenty ad-hoc splits on degree, SCC
size and density whose leaves mix accept and reject with no coherent structure ---
because ``is this argument \pred{in} in \emph{some} stable extension?'' is a
global property of the extension set that local features cannot resolve, and the
target is a near-even split the learner ends up fitting to noise. The contrast is
the paper's thesis in miniature: a feature classifier yields a legible rule only
where the semantics is functional, and an incoherent threshold-tangle where it is
not --- precisely where the generative answer-set formulation, whose answer sets
\emph{are} the extensions, is needed --- and reaches exact clean-label
recovery (Section~\ref{sec:exp1}), with the curated encodings proved correct
outright (Section~\ref{sec:mech}).

\textbf{(b)}~Credulous-stable: one clean rule, then a tangle of $\sim$20 splits

\paragraph{Compactness.} The learned encodings are small: two to three rules
per semantics plus the shared background, against reference encodings of up to
$33$ rules (preferred). Every learned theory in this paper fits --- and is
displayed --- on a fraction of a page.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Computing semantics.} Reduction-based solvers, notably the ASPARTIX
family~\citep{egly2010}, encode each semantics as an ASP program; the ICCMA
competitions~\citep{iccma23} track the state of the art. We invert the
direction: encodings are induced from examples and then \emph{proved}
equivalent to the references.

\paragraph{Learning and argumentation.} A large and growing body of work learns
to \emph{predict} acceptance with neural networks: AGNN~\citep{craandijkbex2020},
graph-convolutional and attention models~\citep{kuhlmann2019,cibier2024gnn},
heterogeneous GNNs for assumption-based frameworks~\citep{gehlot2025hetgnn}, and
the broader argumentation-and-machine-learning line surveyed by
\citet{rago2024argml}. These yield accurate but black-box predictors tied to a
fixed, known semantics --- the data-efficiency and interpretability gap
Section~\ref{sec:eval} quantifies. The closest symbolic line learns argumentation
\emph{frameworks} rather than semantics: \citet{deangelis2024aba}, building on the
ABA-learning programme of \citet{proietti2024aba}, induce assumption-based
framework structure (rules, assumptions, contraries) from labelled examples via
ASP, under a fixed (brave/stable) notion of acceptance. That is the
\emph{inverse} of our problem --- they learn the framework given a semantics; we
learn the semantics given frameworks --- and to our knowledge no prior work
induces the acceptability semantics itself, from labelled extensions, as an
explicit, reusable answer-set program proved equivalent to the reference
encodings, which is our contribution.

\paragraph{Other learning--argumentation lines.} Three older strands deserve
explicit demarcation. Argument-based machine learning~\citep{mozina2007}
uses expert-supplied \emph{arguments} to constrain a classifier's hypothesis
search: the argumentation semantics is fixed and nothing about it is learned.
The neural-symbolic line of \citet{garcez2005vaf} encodes value-based
acceptance computation in a trainable network --- closest to us in covering
VAF, but the learned policy lives in connection weights, not in an explicit,
inspectable program. Probabilistic and neuro-argumentative learning
\citep{riveret2016learning} induces attack structure or acceptance
\emph{degrees} from labellings under a fixed semantics. None of the three
outputs the acceptability semantics itself as a logic program, and none
proves what it learns equivalent to a reference.

\paragraph{ILP / LAS.} We build directly on ILASP's context-dependent,
noise-tolerant learning~\citep{law2016,law2018,law2019,law2018phd} and
FastLAS~\citep{law2020}. The verifier reformulation (B4) relates to
observational predicate learning limits discussed by \citet{law2020}.
Popper~\citep{cropper2021popper} and Aleph~\citep{srinivasan2001aleph}
learn programs evaluated in a single intended model, whereas the target here
is a program whose stable models \emph{enumerate} the extensions --- brave
induction~\citep{sakama2009brave}, which LAS strictly
generalizes~\citep{law2018phd,law2019}; Section~\ref{sec:eval} explains why
noise tolerance is not the discriminator and evaluates the per-argument
projection of the task with strong feature-based learners instead.

\paragraph{Human argumentation.} Behavioural studies of abstract argumentation
begin with reinstatement~\citep{rahwan2010} and develop through the
catalogue-comparison methodology of \citet{cramer2018,guillaume2022}, which our
Experiment~2 re-analyzes. A parallel strand \emph{posits} new formalisms
designed to fit (or normatively refine) judgement --- SCF2~\citep{cramer2019scf2},
the cognitively motivated proposals discussed by \citet{cramer2023cognitive},
and, on the attack-load axis specifically, graded
acceptability~\citep{grossi2019graded} and ranking-based
semantics~\citep{amgoud2013ranking,bonzon2016ranking} --- selecting or
hand-crafting a formalism that best matches behaviour. Our approach is
complementary and, we believe, novel in kind: rather than positing a semantics,
we \emph{induce} the acceptance policy as an explicit, inspectable rule set from
the behaviour itself (Section~\ref{sec:humansem}); the ``smoke/fire'' distrust of
heavily attacked arguments emerges as a learner-surfaced candidate rule
rather than a posited principle (its fold stability and within-corpus support
are limited; Section~\ref{sec:humansem}). Our further contribution is methodological (a leakage-free, gated,
calibrated ILP protocol with directional statistics) and substantive (the
feature-not-policy resolution of contextuality and the negative result on SCC
vocabulary).

\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{sec:concl}

We asked whether argumentation semantics can be learned (Q1), trusted (Q2),
learned robustly (Q3), and used as an instrument on human data (Q4). The
answers: \emph{yes} --- one background and hypothesis space covers AAF and BAF
with learning experiments and theorems, ABA via a corrected translation, and
VAF with an equivalence theorem of its own; \emph{provably} --- with
equivalence theorems for abstract, bipolar, and value-based frameworks and an
exact characterization of the preferred construction, an analysis sharp enough to
uncover and repair a published defect; \emph{yes, within a mapped envelope} ---
near-perfect recovery from clean data at $30$--$40$ examples per class, noise
as the binding but data-recoverable constraint (amplified by label
incompleteness), imbalance immaterial, clean-label recovery reproduced across
sparse, self-attacking, and larger ($n{\le}12$) generators and two further
framework families, with self-attacking preferred the one identified boundary;
and \emph{yes} --- on
human data the calibrated learner both confirms the CF2 ceiling under a
stronger-than-before test and returns what the catalogue cannot: a legible
human acceptance policy --- committal reinstatement and cycle caution, plus
a \emph{candidate} non-Dungian distrust of heavily attacked arguments --- and
a principled rejection of per-graph semantics.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The synthetic envelope is mapped on random digraphs of
moderate size ($n\le12$). Clean-label recovery reproduces across the sparse,
self-attacking, and larger generators; under noise, larger frameworks carry
heavier timeout censoring ($25/160$ runs hit the cap, so their noisy recovery is
read as suggestive), and \emph{preferred on self-attacking frameworks} degrades
(PRF $0.42$--$0.71$ under noise) because self-attacks break the conflict-freeness
and subset-maximality the construction leans on --- a specific, mechanistically
understood boundary rather than a general failure. Grounded semantics was
scoped out of the learnability study for a diagnosed reason, and that
diagnosis is now confirmed and closed (\ref{app:grd}): grounded is a
functional target that the campaign's unified guess-and-check background
cannot express, and with a definite learning background the identical harness
recovers it exactly --- $1.000$ at $f\ge20$ on both sparse and dense pools
($0.918$ at $f{=}10$ sparse, $1.000$ at $f{=}10$ dense), learning the
two-rule program $\mathit{out}(X)\leftarrow\mathit{defeated}(X)$;
$\mathit{in}(X)\leftarrow\mathit{supported}(X)$ in $29$ of $30$ folds. The
principled scope boundary of Experiment~1 is the single shared background,
not learnability; the noise robustness of the definite formulation remains
future work. VAF is covered by the unified background, its
specialization (Remark~\ref{rem:spec}), and the equivalence theorem of
Section~\ref{sec:thmvaf}, but carries no learning experiments of its own
here. $\sigma_H$ describes pooled behaviour on seven
graphs; its stable core recurs in $15$--$20$ of $20$ cross-seed folds but its
smoke/fire hedge in only $5$ of $20$, with an antecedent realized exactly
once in the corpus --- a falsifiable candidate awaiting new stimuli; its
auxiliary vocabulary was stress-tested by leave-one-condition-out
re-selection --- the reinstatement family is re-chosen in $6$ of $7$ folds,
but the in-corpus $+7.7$ gain does not transfer to held-out conditions
(pooled $+2.6$ points, $p{=}.56$) --- so the vocabulary-level claims are
in-corpus descriptions pending pre-registered replication, and the contextual-reasoning test is well-powered only for the
cyclic condition G (the per-condition nulls elsewhere rest on $5$--$11$
held-out cells each). Committed-only accuracy peaks in the mid-$60$s against a
CF2 ceiling near $80\%$: closing that residue likely requires per-participant
modelling, not better features.

\paragraph{Future work.} Three directions follow directly. \emph{Custom
semantics at scale}: the machinery is agnostic to the target; learning
domain-specific acceptance policies (legal, medical, deliberative) from expert
labellings is now an engineering exercise with a calibration methodology
attached. \emph{Equivalence checking inside the loop}: because the learned
cores are tight, hypothesis-versus-target equivalence reduces to validity of
completion formulas (Section~\ref{sec:bg-verif}); running that check inside
learning --- rejecting hypotheses not equivalent to any admissible target ---
would turn the post-hoc theorems of Section~\ref{sec:mech} --- whose
run-level counterpart the exactness audit of Section~\ref{sec:exp1surfaces}
already realizes offline --- into a learn-and-prove loop. \emph{Dialogue}: combining the learned human policy
$\sigma_H$ with argument-mining front ends to predict, and intervene on, real
deliberation.

\section{Proofs of the equivalence theorems}
\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Preliminaries}
\label{app:prelim}

Fix a finite AAF $F=(A,R)$, encoded as in Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}: facts
$\pred{arg}(a)$ for every $a\in A$ and $\pred{att}(b,a)$ for every
$(b,a)\in R$, and no other constants. All programs are grounded over $A$;
input atoms ($\pred{arg}$, $\pred{att}$) are fixed by the facts and we
partially evaluate them away, so completions are propositional theories over
the atoms $\pred{in}(a)$, $\pred{out}(a)$, $\pred{defeated}(a)$,
$\pred{not\_defended}(a)$ for $a\in A$. For $a\in A$ write
$\Att(a)=\{b\in A:(b,a)\in R\}$, and read an empty disjunction as $\bot$.

Every program compared below is \emph{tight}: the only \emph{positive}
dependencies are $\pred{defeated}$ on $\pred{in}$ (and input atoms),
$\pred{not\_defended}$ on input atoms, $\pred{out}$ on
$\pred{defeated}$/$\pred{not\_defended}$, and $\pred{in}$ on $\pred{arg}$;
every other dependency is through a negated literal, so the positive
dependency graph is acyclic. By Fages'
theorem~\citep{fages1994,erdem2003}, the answer sets of each program are
exactly the models of its Clark completion, where the completion of an atom
with no defining rules is its negation and constraints are added as
implications to $\bot$.

The completion of the definitional context $\Daaf$ contributes, for every
$a\in A$:
\begin{align}
\pred{defeated}(a) &\;\leftrightarrow\; \textstyle\bigvee_{b\in \Att(a)}\pred{in}(b),
\tag{D1}\\
\pred{not\_defended}(a) &\;\leftrightarrow\; \textstyle\bigvee_{b\in \Att(a)}\neg\pred{defeated}(b).
\tag{D2}
\end{align}
The ASPARTIX references $S_\sigma$ all share the guessing part, whose
completion contributes, for every $a\in A$ (using that $\pred{arg}(a)$ is
true):
\begin{align}
\pred{in}(a) \;\leftrightarrow\; \neg\pred{out}(a),
\tag{G}
\end{align}
(the rule pair \texttt{in(X) :- not out(X), arg(X)} /
\texttt{out(X) :- not in(X), arg(X)} completes to
$\pred{in}(a)\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{out}(a)$ and
$\pred{out}(a)\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{in}(a)$, which coincide), together
with (D1), with (D2) where \pred{not\_defended} occurs ($\sigma\in\{$admissible,
complete$\}$), and with the constraints
\begin{align}
&\neg\bigl(\pred{in}(a)\wedge\pred{in}(b)\bigr)
&&\text{for every }(a,b)\in R,
\tag{CF}\\
&\pred{out}(a)\rightarrow\pred{defeated}(a)
&&\text{for every }a\in A\ \ (\sigma=\text{stable}),
\tag{ST}\\
&\pred{in}(a)\rightarrow\neg\pred{not\_defended}(a)
&&\text{for every }a\in A\ \ (\sigma\in\{\text{adm},\text{cmp}\}),
\tag{AD}\\
&\pred{out}(a)\rightarrow\pred{not\_defended}(a)
&&\text{for every }a\in A\ \ (\sigma=\text{complete}).
\tag{CO}
\end{align}
The learned programs $P_\sigma\cup\Daaf$ complete to (D1), (D2), and:
\begin{align*}
\text{stable:}&&
\pred{out}(a)&\leftrightarrow\pred{defeated}(a), &
\pred{in}(a)&\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{out}(a);\\
\text{admissible:}&&
\pred{out}(a)&\leftrightarrow\pred{defeated}(a)\vee\neg\pred{in}(a), &
\pred{in}(a)&\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{out}(a)\wedge\neg\pred{not\_defended}(a);\\
\text{complete:}&&
\pred{out}(a)&\leftrightarrow\pred{not\_defended}(a), &
\pred{in}(a)&\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{out}(a)\wedge\neg\pred{defeated}(a).
\end{align*}
In every model of either side, $\pred{defeated}$ and $\pred{not\_defended}$
are determined by $\pred{in}$ via (D1)--(D2), and (as the proofs below show)
$\pred{out}$ is determined as well; so exhibiting the same
$\{\pred{in},\pred{out}\}$-restrictions on both sides establishes the
bijection claimed in Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}. ($S_{\mathrm{stb}}$ does not
mention \pred{not\_defended}; its models extend uniquely by (D2), which we do
tacitly.) Finally, the identification of the $\pred{in}$-projections with the
$\sigma$-extensions is the correctness of the ASPARTIX
references~\citep{egly2010}.

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf}}

\paragraph{Stable}
($\Rightarrow$) Let $M$ be a model of the learned completion. (G) is the
learned $\pred{in}$-equation. (ST): immediate from
$\pred{out}(a)\leftrightarrow\pred{defeated}(a)$. (CF): suppose $(a,b)\in R$,
$\pred{in}(a)$, $\pred{in}(b)$. By (D1) $\pred{defeated}(b)$; by the learned
$\pred{out}$-equation $\pred{out}(b)$; by (G) $\neg\pred{in}(b)$ ---
contradiction.
($\Leftarrow$) Let $N$ be a model of the reference completion. The learned
$\pred{in}$-equation is (G). For
$\pred{out}(a)\leftrightarrow\pred{defeated}(a)$: left to right is (ST); right
to left, if $\pred{defeated}(a)$ then $\pred{in}(b)$ for some $b\in\Att(a)$
by (D1), so $\pred{in}(a)$ would violate (CF) at $(b,a)\in R$; hence
$\neg\pred{in}(a)$, and $\pred{out}(a)$ by (G).\hfill$\lozenge$

\paragraph{Admissible}
($\Rightarrow$) (G): if $\pred{in}(a)$ then $\neg\pred{out}(a)$ by the learned
$\pred{in}$-equation; if $\neg\pred{out}(a)$ then, by the learned
$\pred{out}$-equation, $\neg\pred{defeated}(a)$ \emph{and} $\pred{in}(a)$.
(AD): immediate from the learned $\pred{in}$-equation. (CF): if $(a,b)\in R$
with $\pred{in}(a),\pred{in}(b)$, then $\pred{defeated}(b)$ by (D1), so
$\pred{out}(b)$ by the learned $\pred{out}$-equation, so $\neg\pred{in}(b)$
by (G) --- contradiction.
($\Leftarrow$) Learned $\pred{out}$-equation
$\pred{out}(a)\leftrightarrow\pred{defeated}(a)\vee\neg\pred{in}(a)$: right to
left, $\neg\pred{in}(a)$ gives $\pred{out}(a)$ by (G), and
$\pred{defeated}(a)$ gives $\neg\pred{in}(a)$ by (CF) as in the stable case;
left to right, $\pred{out}(a)$ gives $\neg\pred{in}(a)$ by (G). Learned
$\pred{in}$-equation
$\pred{in}(a)\leftrightarrow\neg\pred{out}(a)\wedge\neg\pred{not\_defended}(a)$:
left to right is (G) plus (AD); right to left, $\neg\pred{out}(a)$ already
gives $\pred{in}(a)$ by (G).\hfill$\lozenge$

\paragraph{Complete}
($\Rightarrow$) (G): $\pred{in}(a)\rightarrow\neg\pred{out}(a)$ is part of the
learned $\pred{in}$-equation. Conversely let $\neg\pred{out}(a)$; then
$\neg\pred{not\_defended}(a)$ by the learned $\pred{out}$-equation, i.e.\ by
(D2) every $b\in\Att(a)$ has $\pred{defeated}(b)$. Suppose
$\pred{defeated}(a)$: some $b\in\Att(a)$ has $\pred{in}(b)$, and the learned
$\pred{in}$-equation gives $\neg\pred{defeated}(b)$ --- contradicting
$\pred{defeated}(b)$. So $\neg\pred{defeated}(a)$, and $\pred{in}(a)$ follows
from the learned $\pred{in}$-equation. (CF): $(a,b)\in R$ with
$\pred{in}(a),\pred{in}(b)$ gives $\pred{defeated}(b)$ by (D1) but
$\neg\pred{defeated}(b)$ from the learned $\pred{in}$-equation ---
contradiction. (AD): $\pred{not\_defended}(a)$ gives $\pred{out}(a)$
(learned $\pred{out}$-equation), gives $\neg\pred{in}(a)$ ((G), just
established). (CO): immediate from the learned $\pred{out}$-equation.
($\Leftarrow$) Learned $\pred{out}$-equation
$\pred{out}(a)\leftrightarrow\pred{not\_defended}(a)$: left to right is (CO);
right to left, $\pred{not\_defended}(a)$ gives $\neg\pred{in}(a)$ by (AD),
gives $\pred{out}(a)$ by (G). Learned $\pred{in}$-equation: left to right,
$\pred{in}(a)$ gives $\neg\pred{out}(a)$ by (G) and $\neg\pred{defeated}(a)$
by (CF)+(D1) as before; right to left, $\neg\pred{out}(a)$ gives
$\pred{in}(a)$ by (G).\hfill$\lozenge$

\medskip
\noindent In each case the two completions have the same models on
$\{\pred{in},\pred{out}\}$, and each model is determined by its
$\pred{in}$-part; Theorem~\ref{thm:aaf} follows.\hfill$\square$

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:baf}}

Ground over the same finite domain, now with input relations
$\pred{arg},\pred{att},\pred{support},\mathit{supp}$, under the standing
stipulation of the theorem that every constant is an argument (the case
proofs use it wherever (G) or a learned $\pred{in}$-equation is invoked).
Both programs contain
the \emph{same} three defining rules for \pred{defeat} (reading
$\mathit{supp}$), so both completions contain the same equation
\[
\pred{defeat}(b,a)\;\leftrightarrow\;
\pred{att}(b,a)\;\vee\;
\textstyle\bigvee_{c}\bigl(\pred{att}(c,a)\wedge\mathit{supp}(b,c)\bigr)
\;\vee\;
\textstyle\bigvee_{c}\bigl(\pred{att}(b,c)\wedge\mathit{supp}(c,a)\bigr),
\]
whose right-hand side is a formula of \emph{input} atoms only. Hence in any
model, on either side, \pred{defeat} denotes the same relation
$R^{\mathit{supp}}=\{(b,a): \text{the right-hand side holds}\}$, and
\pred{defeat} is not positively recursive, so both programs remain tight.
The remaining rules are exactly those of the AAF case with \pred{att}
replaced by \pred{defeat}: (D1), (D2), and (CF) now read
$\Att(a)=\{b:(b,a)\in R^{\mathit{supp}}\}$. The three case proofs above use
the attack relation only through (D1), (D2), and (CF) --- never through the
input facts directly --- so they apply verbatim with $R$ replaced by
$R^{\mathit{supp}}$. (As noted in the main text, the two premises on
$\mathit{supp}$ are not used.)\hfill$\square$

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:vaf}}

Ground over the finite domain with input relations
$\pred{arg},\pred{att},\pred{val},\pred{valpref},\pred{pref}$, under the
standing stipulation that every constant is an argument. Both programs
contain the same single defining rule for \pred{defeat}, so both completions
contain the equation
\[
\pred{defeat}(b,a)\;\leftrightarrow\;
\pred{att}(b,a)\wedge\neg\pred{pref}(a,b),
\]
whose right-hand side is a formula of input atoms only; in any model, on
either side, \pred{defeat} denotes the same relation
$R^{\pred{pref}}=\{(b,a):\pred{att}(b,a)\wedge\neg\pred{pref}(a,b)\}$. The
negative \pred{pref} literal contributes no positive dependency, so both
programs remain tight. The remaining rules are exactly those of the AAF case
with \pred{att} replaced by \pred{defeat}; the three case proofs above use
the attack relation only through (D1), (D2), and (CF), so they apply verbatim
with $R$ replaced by $R^{\pred{pref}}$.\hfill$\square$

\section{Grounded semantics: exact recovery with a definite learning background}
\label{app:grd}

Grounded semantics was scoped out of Experiment~1 (footnote of
Section~\ref{sec:bg-af}); this appendix closes that loop by exhibiting the
mechanism and the fix. The obstacle is formulational, not informational.
Grounded is a \emph{functional} target: every framework has exactly one
grounded labelling, the subset-minimal complete one. The campaign's unified
learning background is a guess-and-check theory --- choice rules
$0\,\{\mathit{in}(X)\}\,1$ and $0\,\{\mathit{out}(X)\}\,1$ generate candidate
labellings and the learned hypothesis prunes them. Under that background
every labelling is an answer set until constraints remove it, so only
constraint-expressible semantics are learnable; subset-minimality is not
expressible as a constraint theory over this vocabulary, and the grounded
task degenerates. Replacing the learning background with a \emph{definite}
one (\texttt{config/bg\_nochoice\_grd.lp}: the same derived vocabulary, no
choice rules, so the hypothesis must \emph{derive}
$\mathit{in}/\mathit{out}$) removes the obstacle while leaving every other
component of the harness untouched.

We ran grounded through the standard pipeline --- identical grouped $5$-fold
framework-disjoint cross-validation, oracle negatives, exact-model evaluation
on the bare framework, standard configuration files --- on two committed
$500$-AAF pools: the dense Experiment~1 pool (grounded extension empty in
$68\%$ of frameworks) and the sparse breadth pool (empty in $26\%$), with
clean, complete labellings and $f\in\{10,20,40\}$ example pairs.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\hline
Exact recovery (mean over $5$ folds) & $f{=}10$ & $f{=}20$ & $f{=}40$\\
\hline
dense pool  & $1.000$ & $1.000$ & $1.000$\\
sparse pool & $0.918$ & $1.000$ & $1.000$\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

All $30$ ILASP runs succeeded (no timeout, no UNSAT; $1.1$--$2.0$\,s training
each), and $29$ of $30$ learned the same two-rule program,
\[
\mathit{out}(X) \leftarrow \mathit{defeated}(X), \qquad
\mathit{in}(X) \leftarrow \mathit{supported}(X),
\]
which is exactly the definite core of the grounded fixpoint over the
background's derived vocabulary ($\mathit{defeated}$: attacked by an accepted
argument; $\mathit{supported}$: all attackers rejected). The single imperfect
fold (sparse, $f{=}10$) learned the over-general
$\mathit{out}(X) \leftarrow \mathit{not\_defended}(X)$, an alternative
consistent with its ten training examples. Notably, exact recovery is perfect
on the \emph{dense} pool even at $f{=}10$: empty-extension prevalence was
never the binding factor once the background fits the target. The principled
scope boundary of Experiment~1 is therefore the single unified
guess-and-check background shared by all four campaign semantics, not the
learnability of grounded. This appendix is confined to clean labels; the
noise robustness of the definite formulation (where a single mislabelled
argument can break a definite derivation chain) remains future work.
Per-fold results are committed under \texttt{data/grd\_appendix/}; the driver
is \texttt{experiments/run\_grd\_appendix.py}.
